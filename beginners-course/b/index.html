Let's talk about the drawer.

You know the one. Everybody's got one.

Yours might be in the kitchen. Might be in the spare bedroom. Might be in the cupboard under the stairs. But somewhere in your home, right now, there is a drawer - or a shelf, or a bag tucked behind the sofa - full of art supplies you bought with the best of intentions.

Pencils. Maybe a sketchbook with the first three pages used and the rest blank. A set of watercolours still in the plastic. A "beginner's drawing kit" from that time you told yourself, this year, I'm finally going to learn.

And then you didn't.

Not because you're lazy. Not because you didn't want it badly enough. You wanted it so much it actually hurt a little when you put those supplies away and closed the drawer.

The Talent Lie

Here's something most art teachers will never tell you, because their entire business model depends on you believing the opposite:

Talent is not the thing that separates people who sketch from people who don't.

Method is.

Think about it. You learned to drive a car. You learned to cook a meal. You learned to use a smartphone that would have baffled the brightest engineers of 1990. None of those things are "natural talents." They're systems. Someone showed you the steps. You followed them. You got better.

Sketching works exactly the same way.

The problem is that most art instruction doesn't teach you a system. It teaches you to copy what the instructor is doing. And when the instructor stops, so do you - because you were never given a framework you could use on your own.

That's the difference. Not talent. Not age. Not some gift you either have or don't.

A system you can follow. That's all that was ever missing.

The Six Frameworks That Make It Stick

Most courses teach you what to draw. Ian teaches you how to see. These six named frameworks are the system:

The Layers of Looking

How to build any sketch in five to seven stages from blank page to finished piece. Each layer has a specific job. No guessing. No paralysis.

The Four Pillars

Perspective, Composition, Detail, Tonal Value. The structural foundation every sketch stands on. Once you understand these four things, you can sketch anything.

The Editor's Eye

What to leave out matters more than what you put in. A five-filter system for deciding what belongs in your sketch and what doesn't. This is the framework that turns cluttered scenes into clean, compelling art.

The Colour Family

Three colours create infinite harmony. No colour-matching anxiety. No overwhelm at the paint aisle. Three colours. That's it.

The Boldness Paradox

The moment you want to pull back is the moment to push forward. This one changes more than your sketching.

The Storytelling Sketch

You're not drawing buildings. You're telling the story of a place. This is where sketching becomes personal. Where your sketches stop looking like everyone else's and start looking like yours.

These aren't abstract concepts. They're named, repeatable systems you'll use every single time you pick up a pencil.
