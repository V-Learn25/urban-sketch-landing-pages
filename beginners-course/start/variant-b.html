What the First Twenty Minutes Feel Like

Let me show you what that advantage looks like in practice.

It's evening. Quiet house. The day is done. You're at the kitchen table — cup of tea, sketchbook open, lamp on. On the screen there's me, standing in front of a beautiful old stone bridge in Shrewsbury. Three arches. Weathered stone. Light on the water.

It looks impossible.

And I say: "Forget the bridge. Forget the whole thing. Just look at this one shape here — see it? That rectangle? Just draw that."

So you draw it.

Your hand is tight on the pencil. The line wobbles. It doesn't look like much. But it's a rectangle. On your paper. And you did it.

"Good," I say. "Now this shape next to it. See how it sits? A bit taller. Slightly narrower."

You draw that one. Then the next. Then an arch underneath. And somewhere between the third shape and the fifth, something changes in your hand. You didn't tell it to. The grip softens. The pencil moves a little faster. The shapes start to connect and you are not thinking about whether you are doing it right because my voice is still there, calm and unhurried, and your hand keeps moving.

You glance down.

There's a bridge on your page.

Not a perfect bridge. Not a photograph. A wobbly, sketchy, imperfect, unmistakably real-looking bridge. Made of six or seven simple shapes that you drew, one at a time, with your own hand, in the last fifteen minutes.

Your tea went cold. You didn't notice.

The Layer Cake Method

One layer at a time. Pencil shapes first — just the skeleton of what you see. Then ink — the confident lines that bring the whole thing alive. Then colour — not filling in, but painting light.

Each layer has a single job. Each layer builds on the last. Nothing complicated. Nothing mysterious. Just a teacher showing you which shape to draw next.

And by the end of that first sitting, you have a finished sketch. A real one. The English Bridge, in pencil and ink and watercolour, sitting on your kitchen table. Something you could pin on the wall. Something you made.

That tightness in your stomach — the one that has been saying you can't do this for thirty years — loosens. Just a little. Just enough for you to turn the page and think: What's next?

The Editor's Eye

What you leave out defines a sketch more than what you put in. You start making choices. This detail matters. That one doesn't. This shadow tells the story. That drainpipe adds nothing.

Your hand moves faster because it isn't trying to record everything — it's selecting. Editing. Deciding.

And the sketch that comes out of this workshop has something the first one didn't. Not more skill. More confidence. You can see it in the lines. Looser. Bolder. The marks of someone who is starting to trust her own eye.

Workshop 4. Wyle Cop. The final workshop. Everything together. And this is where your worst enemy becomes your greatest asset.

Your perfectionism means you care about quality. That you refuse to accept sloppy work. That something in you demands excellence. Those aren't weaknesses. They're only a problem in sketching because nobody ever told you the secret:

The Boldness Paradox

The instinct to pull back is the signal to push forward. You'll reach a point in the sketch — usually during the colour stage — and your instinct will scream: Stop. Be careful. You're about to ruin it.

And instead of stopping, you push. A bold line. A splash of colour. And it sings. Not despite the imperfection. Because of it.

Three colours. Mixed in pairs. Infinite palette. No more paralysis in the art shop. No more muddy colours. No more buying thirty tubes of paint when three will do.
