The Real Reason Landscape Sketches Fall Flat (and It's Not What You Think)

Here's what happens when most people try to sketch a landscape. You sit down somewhere beautiful. A country lane. A river winding through a valley. Rolling hills dotted with stone walls and old farm gates.

Your brain does what it always does: it tries to take in everything. Every leaf. Every ripple. Every stone in the wall. Every blade of grass bending in the wind.

And that's when the trouble starts. Your pencil hovers over the page. You don't know where to begin. You make a few marks. They feel stiff. Flat. The more you draw, the worse it gets.

The problem was never your hands. It was your eyes.

You were trying to draw what you saw. And what you saw was chaos - a beautiful, overwhelming, impossible-to-capture mess of green and light and texture.

Traditional drawing instruction says "look more carefully." Ian Fennelly says the opposite.

He says stop looking at the details. Start looking at the layers.

The Layers of Looking Method

Most sketching advice treats landscapes like still life. Observe closely. Measure proportions. Get the perspective right. That works fine for a bowl of fruit on a kitchen table. It falls apart completely when you're staring at three miles of mountainous countryside.

What Ian discovered after 30-plus years of sketching on location around the world is that landscapes don't organise themselves by perspective. They organise themselves by depth.

Foreground→ Midground→ Background

Three layers. That's it. And within each layer, you're only looking for five things:

Shape→ Line→ Colour→ Pattern→ Texture

Think of it like building a cake from the bottom up. Each layer supports the next. You're not trying to capture the whole scene at once. You're stacking simple layers, one on top of another, until the landscape appears on the page - with air between the layers, with atmosphere in the distance, with a foreground that feels close enough to touch and mountains that feel miles away.

Your hand follows your eye. And because your eye now knows exactly where to look and what to ignore, your hand moves faster, looser, and with a kind of confidence that surprises you.
